A. P. J. Abdul Kalam

The distinguished Indian scientist and politician contributed significantly to India's missile and nuclear weapons programs and played a pivotal role in their development.

About the Legend

☛ Born on October 15, 1931, in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, India, the full name of A.P.J. Abdul Kalam was Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam.

☛ From 2002 to 2007, he held the position of the 11th President of India.

☛ After obtaining a degree in aeronautical engineering from the Madras Institute of Technology, Kalam became a part of the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) in 1958.

☛He shifted to the Indian Space Research Organisation in 1969 and served as the project director for the SLV-III. It was the first satellite launch vehicle that India had independently designed and manufactured.

☛ After rejoining DRDO in 1982, Kalam strategized and executed a program that resulted in the development of several effective missiles, a feat that led to him being famously referred to as the “Missile Man.”

☛ One of the achievements was the development of Agni, India's inaugural intermediate-range ballistic missile that integrated some features of the SLV-III and was first tested in 1989.

☛ He played a crucial part in organizing, providing technical expertise, and navigating the political landscape during India's Pokhran-II nuclear tests in 1998, which were the first tests conducted by India since its initial nuclear test in 1974.

☛ Between 1992 and 1997, Kalam held the position of scientific adviser to the defense minister. Later, he was appointed as the principal scientific adviser (1999-2001) to the government, with the status of a cabinet minister.

☛ In the year 1998, India's nuclear weapons tests were a significant event that established Kalam as a national hero due to his notable involvement, but it also caused considerable concern in the global community. These tests cemented India's position as a nuclear power.

☛ Kalam introduced a nationwide strategy named Technology Vision 2020 in 1998, with the aim of converting India into a developed nation within two decades, by providing a roadmap for social and economic transformation. This plan involved multiple initiatives such as improving agricultural output, promoting technology as a driver for economic progress, and enhancing the reach of healthcare and education.

☛ Kalam received 7 honorary doctorates from 40 universities. The Government of India honoured him with the Padma Bhushan in 1981 and the Padma Vibhushan in 1990 for his work with ISRO and DRDO and his role as a scientific advisor to the Government.

☛ Kalam was awarded the Bharat Ratna, the highest civilian honour in India, in 1997 for his efforts in advancing scientific research and modernizing defense technology in the country.

☛ The National Space Society awarded him the Von Braun Award in 2013, which acknowledges exceptional management and leadership of a space-related project.

☛ On the 27th of July 2015, at the age of 83, Kalam suffered a cardiac arrest and passed away after collapsing while giving a speech at the Indian Institute of Management Shillong.

☛ Wheeler Island, a national missile test site in Odisha, was renamed Kalam Island in September 2015.

☛ A prominent road in New Delhi was renamed from Aurangzeb Road to Dr APJ Abdul Kalam Road in August 2015.

☛ A plant species that was newly discovered was named Drypetes kalamii by scientists from the Botanical Survey of India in February 2018, as a tribute to him.
